Meaning of Sound

Sound is a form of energy produced by vibrating bodies. Anything that vibrates (e.g. tuning fork, drum, vocal cords, speakers) can produce sound. When an object vibrates, it causes the surrounding air particles to vibrate, creating a wave that travels to the ear.

Nature of Sound Waves

Sound waves are:
a. Longitudinal Waves:
Particles of the medium vibrate back and forth in the same direction as the wave travels.

They form:
1. Compressions – particles close together
2. Rarefactions – particles spread out

b. Mechanical Waves:
They require a material medium (solid, liquid, or gas). Sound cannot travel through a vacuum.

---

Production and Propagation of Sound

Sound is produced when an object vibrates. These vibrations cause the medium’s particles to oscillate, passing energy from one point to another.
Propagation depends on the medium:
1.Fastest in solids
2.Slower in liquids
3.Slowest in gases

Reason: Particle arrangement and closeness.

Characteristics of Sound Waves

1. Pitch:
Depends on the frequency of the sound wave.
# High frequency → high pitch
# Low frequency → low pitch
Example: A whistle has a higher pitch than a drum.

2. Loudness:
Depends on amplitude of vibration.
#Larger amplitude → louder sound #Smaller amplitude → softer sound

3. Quality (Timbre)
Enables us to distinguish between sounds of the same pitch and loudness from different sources (e.g., piano vs guitar).

Speed of Sound

Speed of sound varies with medium, temperature, and humidity.
# Approximate values:
Air (20°C): 343 m/s
Water: 1500 m/s
Iron: 5000 m/s

Formula:
v = \frac{d}{t}
Where:
v = speed
d = distance
t = time

Reflection of Sound

Sound can bounce off surfaces just like light. This produces:
a. Echo:
A sound heard after reflection from a distant surface.

Conditions for echo:
# Reflecting surface at least 17 m away (since sound needs 0.1 s to be heard separately) Hard, smooth surface
b. Reverberation:
Repeated reflections making sound prolonged or echoey, common in halls and churches.

To reduce reverberation:
a. Use curtains
b. Carpets
c. Sound-absorbing materials

Applications of Sound

1. Ultrasound in medicine (scanning, imaging)
2. SONAR for detecting underwater objects
3. Echo sounding for measuring sea depth
4. Communication
5. Musical instruments

Noise vs Musical Sound

Noise Musical Sound
Unpleasant, irregular vibration Pleasant, regular vibration Example: Traffic noise Example: Piano, violin

Human Hearing Range # Below 20 Hz → Infrasound
# Above 20 kHz → Ultrasound

Differences Between Sound Waves and Light Waves

Sound/Light
1. Mechanical/Electromagnetic
2. Longitudinal/Transverse
3. Needs medium/Can travel in vacuum
4. Slower/Very fast (3×10⁸ m/s)
